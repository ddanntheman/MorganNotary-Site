Estate and trust signings are not like other notarizations. They happen at hospital bedsides, in senior-living communities, around kitchen tables with adult children present. They are almost always emotionally weighty. And they matter enormously — a signature in the wrong place or a missed initial can invalidate a document the family has spent months preparing.

Here is how I approach these signings, and what families should know before the day.

## Before the appointment

**Have the documents ready.** If your attorney prepared them, make sure the full packet is with the signer — not still sitting in the attorney's office. If you are missing pages, we cannot sign.

**Plan for witnesses.** Virginia wills and most trusts require two witnesses in addition to the notary. Those witnesses must be disinterested adults — not beneficiaries, not the signer's spouse. If you do not have two people available, I can bring qualified witnesses with me (included in my estate package pricing).

**Confirm the signer's ID.** A current, government-issued photo ID is required. If the signer's ID is expired or their name has changed (marriage, divorce, etc.), text me ahead of time and we will figure out the workaround — we do not want that surprise at the bedside.

**Know the facility's rules.** Hospitals and senior-living communities often have visiting hours, security check-ins, and parking constraints. Tell me the facility and I will plan accordingly.

## During the signing

I work at the signer's pace. If they need to rest, we rest. If they want to read every page themselves, we read every page. My job is to verify identity, confirm willingness, and direct them to the correct signature and initial locations — not to rush.

I do not explain the legal effect of the document. That is the attorney's job, and it needs to happen before the signing, not at it. If the signer asks a legal question mid-signing, I will pause and recommend calling the attorney.

I also watch for capacity concerns. If a signer is clearly confused, heavily medicated, or unable to answer basic questions about what they are signing, I have a professional and legal duty to decline the notarization. This is rare, but it is how the signing protects the family in the long run.

## After

I leave the family with original signed copies. If the attorney needs originals returned, I can coordinate that directly. If any part of the signing requires follow-up — a missed page, a correction, a late-arriving document — I handle it the same day.

## A note for caregivers

If you are the adult child or caregiver arranging this: thank you for the work you are doing. It is hard. I will do everything I can to make the signing itself one of the easier parts of the week.
