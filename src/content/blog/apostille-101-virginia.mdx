If someone just asked you for an apostille and you have never heard the word before, welcome — you are in the right place.

## What is an apostille?

An apostille is a certificate that authenticates the origin of a public document so that document will be legally recognized in another country. Think of it as an international stamp of approval: the issuing authority (in our case, the Virginia Secretary of State or the U.S. Department of State) verifies that the signature and seal on your document are genuine.

The apostille itself was created by the 1961 Hague Convention. Any country that is a party to that convention — more than 120 countries, including most of Europe, Mexico, and much of Asia — will accept an apostille from another member country without any further authentication.

## When do you need one?

The most common triggers:

- **International marriage** — the other country needs your U.S. birth certificate authenticated
- **Dual citizenship** applications — birth, marriage, and other civil documents
- **Foreign adoptions** — home studies, consent forms, and U.S. background checks
- **Overseas jobs** — diplomas, transcripts, FBI background checks
- **Retirement or residency** abroad — powers of attorney, affidavits
- **International business** — corporate documents, articles of incorporation, powers of attorney

If a form or a lawyer or a foreign consulate has used the word "apostille," there is no shortcut: you need one.

## State vs. federal

Documents issued by Virginia (birth certificates, marriage certificates, Virginia diplomas, Virginia-notarized documents) get a **Virginia apostille** from the Secretary of State in Richmond.

Documents issued by the U.S. federal government (FBI background checks, IRS documents, military records) get a **federal apostille** from the U.S. Department of State in Washington, D.C.

If your document is not originally Virginia- or federally-issued, you may need to have it notarized first, then apostilled. That is where I come in.

## What about non-Hague countries?

Some countries are not party to the Hague Convention (China, the UAE, Vietnam, and several others). For those countries, the process is called **authentication** (or sometimes "legalization") and it goes through additional layers — typically the issuing state, then the U.S. Department of State, then the destination country's embassy or consulate. I handle the state and federal layers and coordinate the embassy step when needed.

## How long does it take?

Virginia state apostilles: 3–5 business days once the document reaches the Secretary of State. Federal apostilles: 5–10 business days. Rush processing is sometimes available — ask when you reach out.
